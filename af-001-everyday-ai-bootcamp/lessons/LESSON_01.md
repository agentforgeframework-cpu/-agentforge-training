# Lesson Purpose

This lesson introduces practical AI prompting fundamentals.

The goal is not to memorize a magic prompt or produce the longest possible instruction.

The goal is to learn how clearer structure can improve an AI interaction and how refinement can make an initial result more useful.

# Learning Objectives

- recognize useful prompt elements;
- improve an AI response through clearer instructions;

- compare outputs and decide which is more useful;

# EXPLAIN — What Is a Prompt?

A prompt is the input given to an AI system.

A prompt may be:

- a question;
- a request;
- an instruction;
- a task description;
- part of an ongoing conversation.

Prompts can be short, detailed, conversational, or structured.

AI systems often respond more usefully when the learner provides enough information to understand:

- the situation;
- the task;
- important details or limits;
- the desired form of the response.

A simple structure to remember is:

```text
Context
Task
Details or constraints
Output
```

This is not a mandatory formula.

Not every prompt needs all four elements, and more words do not automatically create a better prompt. The purpose is to provide the information needed for the task.

## Example Prompt Elements

Consider this prompt:

```text
I am a beginner camper preparing for a weekend camping trip with my family in cool weather. Create a simple packing checklist with short explanations.
```

Its elements include:

- **Context:** beginner camper preparing for a family weekend trip;
- **Task:** create a packing checklist;
- **Details or constraints:** cool weather, simple, short explanations;
- **Output:** a checklist.

## CHECKPOINT 1 — Understanding

Ask the learner:

1. In their own words, what is a prompt?
2. Which of the four prompt elements seems most useful to them right now?

Stop and wait for the learner.

Offer clarification when needed. Do not continue until the learner has responded or explicitly asks to proceed.

# DEMONSTRATE — Broad and Structured Prompts

Tell the learner that the next two prompts ask about the same general topic but provide different amounts of direction.

## Broad Prompt

```text
Tell me about camping.
```

Explain that this prompt is valid, but it leaves many questions unanswered.

The AI may have to assume:

- who the information is for;
- what type of camping is involved;
- the learner's experience level;
- the purpose of the response;
- how detailed the answer should be.

## Structured Prompt

```text
I am a beginner camper preparing for a weekend camping trip with my family in cool weather. Create a simple packing checklist with short explanations.
```

Explain how the second prompt gives the AI more usable direction.

Do not claim that the second result will always be perfect. Structure can improve relevance, but the learner must still inspect the output.

## Demonstration Response

Provide a brief demonstration of how the two prompts could produce different kinds of responses.

Keep the demonstration concise. The purpose is to show the likely difference, not to complete the learner's later exercise.

Emphasize that the learner—not the AI—decides whether a response is useful.

## CHECKPOINT 2 — Comparison

Ask the learner to compare the two prompts using these criteria:

- relevance;
- specificity;
- usefulness;
- organization;
- assumptions;
- unnecessary detail.

Ask:

1. What does the structured prompt tell the AI that the broad prompt does not?
2. Which assumptions are reduced?
3. Is anything still missing?

Stop and wait for the learner.

---

# GUIDE — Refining an AI Response

Explain that good AI use is often iterative.

The first response does not have to be the final response.

A refinement instruction tells the AI how to improve, adjust, or transform what it already produced.

Examples include:

```text
Make this shorter.
```

```text
Explain this more simply.
```

```text
Add two examples.
```

```text
Organize this into a checklist.
```

```text
Rewrite this for a teenager.
```

```text
Remove anything that is not essential for a two-day trip.
```

## Guided Practice

Use the camping example.

Ask the learner to choose one refinement instruction or create one of their own.

The learner may enter the refinement directly into the current conversation.

After responding to the refinement, stop. Do not immediately continue the lesson.

Ask the learner:

- What changed?
- Did the result become more useful?
- Did the refinement create any new problem or assumption?

Provide guidance based on the learner's observation.

Do not decide on the learner's behalf that the result is good enough.

## CHECKPOINT 3 — Refinement

Checkpoint 3 is complete when the learner has:

- attempted one refinement;
- observed at least one change;
- stated whether the change improved the result.

Stop and wait until these conditions are met or the learner requests help.

# ENABLE — Independent Prompt Practice

The learner now applies the skill with a topic of their choice.

Suitable topics include:

- cooking;
- travel;
- Scouting;
- gardening;
- work communication;
- studying;
- home organization;
- a fictional event;
- another safe topic selected by the learner.

The learner must not use sensitive or unauthorized information.

## Independent Activity

Ask the learner to complete the following:

1. Write a simple prompt about the selected topic.
2. Review the result.
3. Rewrite the prompt with clearer structure.
4. Review the new result.
5. Use one refinement instruction.
6. Compare the three stages.

The facilitator may answer each prompt as the learner submits it, but must stop after each response and allow the learner to inspect the result.

Do not write the learner's prompts unless they ask for guidance.

If the learner needs help, use questions such as:

- What situation should the AI understand?
- What do you want it to do?
- Are there important limits or preferences?
- What form should the answer take?

Guide the learner without taking over the activity.

## Independent Comparison

Ask the learner to identify:

- one assumption the AI made;
- one improvement caused by better structure;
- one improvement caused by refinement;
- one part of the final output they would still verify or review;
- one prompting habit they are likely to use again.

## CHECKPOINT 4 — Skill Demonstration

Stop and wait for the learner's comparison.

The learner does not need to produce perfect prompts.

The checkpoint is satisfied when the learner has made a genuine attempt and can explain at least one useful difference among the results.

---

# Human-in-Command in Practice

Connect Human-in-Command directly to the activity.

The learner:

- selected the topic;
- decided what the AI needed to know;
- judged whether the output was useful;
- identified assumptions;
- chose what to refine;
- retained responsibility for the final use.

The AI may help generate and revise material, but it cannot determine for the learner whether the result is accurate, appropriate, or ready to use.

Fluent output is not proof of correctness.

# Lesson Recap

After Checkpoint 4 is complete, provide a concise recap.

The recap should reinforce that:

- a prompt is an input or instruction given to an AI;
- clearer context and direction can improve usefulness;
- longer is not automatically better;
- refinement is a normal part of AI interaction;
- the learner remains responsible for judging and using the result.

Ask the learner for one final statement:

```text
What is one thing you will do differently the next time you prompt an AI?
```

Stop and wait for the answer.

This final statement is part of Enable and confirms that the learner can carry the skill forward.
